Brand Story

The origin, the metaphor, the soul of the game
The Name

Prompt Quest — an adventure where your words are your superpower. Every quest begins with a blank text box and a wild scenario. What you type determines what happens next. Say something specific and creative? Magic unfolds in 3D. Say something vague? Hilarious chaos. The "quest" isn't to find the right answer — it's to discover that how you ask changes everything. Kids don't know they're learning prompt engineering. They think they're playing the funniest game ever made.

The Magic Theater

Imagine a magical 3D theater where the audience writes the script. The stage has tiny low-poly actors, props, and backdrops — all ready to go. But nothing happens until someone shouts out what should happen next. The more specific the direction, the more amazing the show. Vague directions? The actors improvise — and it's hilarious. Claude is the invisible director. The 3D engine is the stage. The kid is the playwright.

The Inspiration

Built by a dad who wanted to teach his 7-year-old daughter how to work with AI — not through lectures or chatbots, but through play. When she types "launch the dog from a cannon into the birthday cake" and the game actually tries to show that in 3D, the gap between what she imagined and what appears is comedy gold. Success feels earned. Failure feels funny. And every attempt teaches the same lesson: specific words create specific results.

The Audience

Primary — Kids ages 7-11 (Piaget's Concrete Operational stage). They think logically about concrete things, understand cause-and-effect, and are developing metacognition — but they still struggle with pure abstraction. They need to see the results of their words in 3D, not be told about "prompt engineering principles." An 8-year-old can smell anything "for babies" from a mile away. This game has to feel cool, not educational.

Secondary — Parents & educators who want their kids to develop AI literacy through play. The hackathon judges evaluating creative use of Opus 4.6. Anyone who wants to see a kid type "give the monster a hat made of pizza" and watch the 3D game actually do it.

Positioning

What Prompt Quest is — and what it absolutely isn't
Positioning Statement

For kids who will grow up working alongside AI, Prompt Quest is the 3D game that teaches them how — not through lessons or chatbots, but through play. Describe a solution, watch it come alive in a tiny low-poly world, learn that specificity is a superpower. AI is invisible. Fun is the interface.

Prompt Quest Is
●A game first, educational tool second — kids never feel "taught"
●AI as invisible engine — Claude powers it, kids never see a chatbot
●3D low-poly characters that charm both kids AND judges
●Funny fails that teach — mistakes are entertaining, not punishing
●Concrete feedback — "The monster didn't know what cake to eat!" not "Be more specific"
●Outrageously silly — monsters, robots, dinosaurs, octopuses, skeletons
●Instant and reliable — cached responses + fallbacks, never breaks
Prompt Quest Is Not
●ChatGPT with a cute skin — no chat interface, no conversational AI
●A coding tutorial — no code, no syntax, no IDE
●An AI assistant for kids — the AI never talks directly to the child
●Lecturing or preachy — no "good job!" walls of text
●Punishing — failure is always funny, never frustrating
●Baby-ish — no baby colors, no patronizing tone, no "for little kids" feel
●Complex — one task, one input, one magical 3D result
Competitive White Space

Every existing AI-for-kids product is either a chatbot-with-a-skin (Khanmigo, Character.AI) or a block-coding tool (Scratch AI extensions). None of them let kids see their words come alive in real-time 3D. The gap between "type a description" and "watch a 3D scene act it out" doesn't exist anywhere. That's the wedge. Prompt Quest is in a category of one: AI literacy through interactive comedy.

Color Rules
Never use red for failure
Red triggers anxiety in kids. Use yellow/orange for "funny fail" states. Reserve red-tones only for system-level warnings (connection lost), never for game outcomes.
Glow effects, not flat fills
Interactive elements have subtle colored glows (box-shadow with 0.15 alpha). Creates a magical, luminous feeling. Not neon — think fireflies.
3 accent colors max per screen
Let the 3D game scene be the color explosion — the surrounding UI stays calm. Dark canvas + light UI = the game is the star.
WCAG AA on all backgrounds
All text must pass 4.5:1 contrast ratio. Dark text on light UI backgrounds. Light text inside the dark game canvas.

Typography

Kid-readable, game-appropriate, never condescending
Display / Headlines
Monster Birthday Party!

Fredoka SemiBold / Bold (600-700) — a rounded, bouncy display font designed for children's content. Warm, playful, and friendly without being childish. An 8-year-old reads this as "game font." A parent reads it as "well-designed." Perfect overlap.

Sizes: Game title: 48-56px. Task titles: 28-34px. Section headers: 22-26px. Button labels: 16-18px. All 600+ weight.
Body / UI Text
The monster catches the giant cake and does a happy dance! Everyone cheers as confetti fills the room.

Nunito Regular / SemiBold / Bold (400-700) — same rounded sans-serif family for body text. Generous x-height, open letterforms, unambiguous for developing readers. Highly readable at 16px+ on screens.

Minimum: Body 16px. Narration 18-20px. Feedback 14-16px. Never below 14px for any kid-facing text.
Typography Rules for Kids (Ages 7-11)
Minimum 16px body text
7-11 year olds need larger text than adults. 16px floor for readable content.
Line height: 1.6-1.8
Kids' eyes need vertical breathing room. Tight line-height causes reading fatigue.
Short lines: 45-65 characters
Kids lose their place on long lines. Max-width 540px for text blocks.
Weight over size for hierarchy
Use 3-4 sizes max. Vary weight (400, 600, 700) and color for hierarchy.
No ALL CAPS for content
Slows reading speed by 10-15%. OK for tiny labels and badges, never for sentences.
Reading level: Grade 3-4
Short sentences. Common words. "The monster loved the cake!" not "The creature expressed satisfaction."
Font Stack
Display: 'Fredoka', 'Nunito', system-ui, sans-serif
Body: 'Nunito', -apple-system, BlinkMacSystemFont, 'Segoe UI', sans-serif
Fallback: system-ui, sans-serif

Voice & Tone

How Prompt Quest talks to kids, parents, and judges
The Prompt Quest Voice Test

Would a funny, encouraging older sibling say this? If it sounds like a teacher, a textbook, or a children's TV host — rewrite it. If it sounds like the coolest babysitter who turns everything into an adventure — ship it.

Four Voice Pillars
Playfully Encouraging
Every attempt gets celebrated, not judged. "Whoa, the monster just sneezed fire on the cake! Maybe next time tell it to blow out the candles gently?"
Genuinely Funny
Humor through absurd situations and surprising outcomes, never mockery. The game laughs WITH the kid. Funny fails are BETTER than boring successes.
Concretely Helpful
Feedback is about the GAME, not abstract prompting theory. "You forgot to tell the robot how to cross the river!" not "Try to cover all aspects of the problem."
Never Condescending
8-year-olds KNOW when they're being talked down to. No baby talk, no over-simplified language, no "Great job, buddy!" Instead: "That was epic! What if you tried..."
Narrating a Success
✓ Prompt Quest says
"The monster catches the giant cake and does a happy dance! Confetti everywhere!"
✗ Never says
"Wonderful work! You successfully completed the task by providing detailed instructions."
Narrating a Funny Fail
✓ Prompt Quest says
"The robot walks straight into the river. Pizza's soggy. Robot starts doing the robot dance. Not ideal."
✗ Never says
"Oops! That didn't work. Don't worry, mistakes are how we learn! Would you like to try again?"
Giving a Tip
✓ Prompt Quest says
"The robot didn't know how to cross the river! Try telling it what to use — a bridge? A jetpack? A really long pizza?"
✗ Never says
"Remember, good prompts should address all parts of the problem. Try to think about obstacles."
Task Introduction
✓ Prompt Quest says
"A monster is having its first-ever birthday party. It's 30 feet tall, breathes fire, and has never seen a cake before. What could possibly go wrong?"
✗ Never says
"Welcome to the Monster Birthday Party challenge! In this exercise, you will practice writing detailed prompts."
Writing Rules
●Narrations: max 20 words, present tense, one exclamation mark max
●Feedback: max 2 sentences, always game-specific ("the monster" not "your prompt")
●Use "you" rarely — describe what happens in the scene, not what the kid did
●One idea per sentence, active voice, concrete nouns
●Never: "Great job!" "Well done!" "Good try!" — these are empty calories
●Never use the word "prompt" in kid-facing text — say "description" or "plan"
●Never use edu-jargon: "skills," "learning," "practice," "challenge"
●Never use scary words: "wrong," "failed," "error," "mistake"

3D Visual Identity

Low-poly KayKit + Tiny Treats — charming, consistent, production-quality
Art Direction: Low-Poly Charm

Prompt Quest's 3D world is built entirely from KayKit ($99) and Tiny Treats ($20) asset packs — 700+ pre-purchased low-poly models sharing the same gradient-atlas art style. Every character, prop, and environment is CC0 licensed, approximately 1000 triangles each, and rendered with warm Three.js lighting. The visual language is miniature diorama — like a toy playset brought to life. Think Crossy Road meets Untitled Goose Game — simple, charming, and immediately readable.

Characters (34+ rigged models)

5 Adventurers: Rogue, Barbarian, Mage, Knight, Archer — all sharing the same rig and 161 skeletal animations (walk, wave, cheer, jump, crouch, fishing, magic cast, die, sit, dig...).

Skeletons: Own unique animations (bones falling apart, head spinning). Mystery Series 4+5: Space Ranger, Witch, Clown, Robots, Werewolf, Ninja, Superhero — all compatible with the same 161 clips.

All characters are ~1000 tris, use a single 1024x1024 gradient atlas, and swap animations instantly via crossFadeTo transitions.
Environments (12+ packs)

KayKit: Dungeon, Space Base, City, Restaurant, Forest, Furniture, Medieval, Platformer. Tiny Treats: Charming Kitchen, Pretty Park, Fun Playground, Homely House, Bakery, Bedroom, Living Room.

Each task assembles a unique 3D stage from these packs. The Mage's Kitchen uses Charming Kitchen + Restaurant + Bakery props. The Picnic uses Pleasant Picnic + Pretty Park + Forest.

Three.js Rendering
Lighting Rig
AmbientLight(0xffeedd, 0.6) + DirectionalLight(0xffffff, 1.0) with soft shadows. Warm, golden-hour feel. Camera at 45° FOV for a slightly isometric toy-world perspective.
Performance
Low-poly (~1000 tris each). Single 1024px atlas per pack. Max 10 models per scene. Frustum culling. Preloaded per-task with loading bar. Runs at 60fps on any modern browser.
Animation System
AnimationMixer per character. 161 shared clips loaded once. crossFadeTo(0.25s) transitions. Bounce easing for spawns, arc easing for throws, sine for floats. Falls back to Idle if clip missing.
Canvas
1024x576px (16:9), Phaser.Scale.FIT, antialias enabled, SRGB color space. Dark purple background (#1A0533) makes the colorful models pop.
UI Design Principles
Light UI wraps Dark Game
The surrounding React UI uses the light lavender palette. The 3D viewport is dark and magical. This creates a "window into another world" and makes the game the star.
Touch targets: 48px minimum
Kids' motor skills are still developing. Buttons 48px+ tall, 12px+ gaps between interactive elements. The Submit button is 56px tall.
One action per screen
Each screen has ONE obvious thing to do: choose a task, type a plan, press Go. Everything else is visually recessed.
Border radius: 14-22px
Rounded enough to feel friendly. Buttons 14px, cards 22px. Avoid perfect circles on rectangular elements — that reads as "toy."
Sonic Identity

Playful, warm, and never startling — sounds should make kids smile, not flinch. Every audio cue reinforces the "toy world come to life" feeling.

Celebrations
FULL_SUCCESS = confetti pop + crowd cheer + sparkle shimmer. FUNNY_FAIL = cartoon boing + comedy trombone slide. Every outcome gets a satisfying sound — silence is never the response.
UI Interactions
Button press = soft pop (like a bubble). Submit = whoosh launch. Zone enter = magical chime arpeggio. Badge unlock = triumphant sting + sparkle trail. Light, tactile, never harsh.
Ambient World
Village has gentle background music — medieval lute + light percussion. Each zone has a unique ambient loop hinting at its theme (space hum, kitchen sizzle, forest birdsong). Crossfade on zone transition.
Voice & Narration
TTS narration reads scene results aloud — warm, expressive voice at moderate pace. Mute toggle always visible and respected. Audio should enhance, never gatekeep — the game is fully playable silent.

Avoid: harsh buzzes, aggressive alarms, anything that feels like a wrong-answer buzzer. The sound palette matches the visual palette — warm, round, inviting. Think Nintendo rather than game show.

Emotional Design

How every moment should make a kid feel — the real product
Emotional North Star

"I want to try something weird and see what happens."

If a kid feels safe enough to type something silly, curious enough to wonder what will happen, and delighted enough by the result to try again — you've won. Every design decision is measured against: does this make them more or less likely to try something weird?

🚀
Before Submit
Curious + Excited. "What will happen if I type THIS?" The input should feel like a launchpad.
✨
During Animation
Surprise + Anticipation. "Wait... is it actually going to...?" Build slowly enough for wonder.
😂
After Funny Fail
Laughter + "Again!" The fail should be SO funny they show a friend. Comedy > correctness.
🎉
After Full Success
Pride + "Look what I did!" Confetti, 3D characters cheering — this is THEIR creation.
💡
Reading Feedback
"Oh! I should have..." The tip sparks an idea. It's a clue in a mystery, not a grade.
💫
Trying Again
Confidence + Purpose. "This time I'll add the jetpack AND the bridge." Retry = power-up.
The Core Pattern: Failure as Comedy

Failure must be funnier than success. Most educational games make success exciting and failure neutral. Prompt Quest inverts this. When a kid's vague description causes the knight to swing his sword at a space station control panel, that's PEAK entertainment. The laughter dissolves any frustration. The kid WANTS to see other funny outcomes. In trying to make something specific happen, they naturally learn to write better descriptions. The pedagogy is embedded in the comedy.

Accessibility

Every kid deserves to play — no exceptions
Non-Negotiable

10-15% of kids have some form of disability — learning differences, color vision deficiency, motor challenges, attention difficulties. If the game only works for neurotypical kids with perfect vision, it's broken for 1 in 7 players.

Visual
●WCAG AA (4.5:1) contrast for all text
●Color + shape + text — never color alone for meaning
●No flashing >3x/second (seizure risk)
●prefers-reduced-motion respected — tweens apply final values instantly
Motor & Input
●48px minimum touch targets with 12px gaps
●Tab navigation through all elements
●No drag-and-drop, no precision clicks
●No time pressure — no timers, no countdowns
Cognitive
●One task at a time — sequential, clear flow
●Grade 3-4 reading level — short sentences, common words
●Consistent layout — input always in the same place
●Unlimited attempts — no lives, no game over, no penalty
●Spelling doesn't matter — Claude handles misspellings
●Visible mute button — game fully playable with audio off
